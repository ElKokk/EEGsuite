# EEG Measurement Protocol for Tactile Stimulation with Strong Electromagnetic Noise 

# (2025/12/11 \- BEPG)

**1\. Objective**

Establish a robust and reproducible method to isolate the neural response to tactile stimulation (e.g. \~22 Hz and other selected frequencies) while suppressing strong electromagnetic (EM) noise generated by tactors and background noise.

Note that you not chose 25Hz, 50 Hz, 75 Hz, 100 Hz …

Core strategy:

 **baseline → epoching → normalization → contrasting**

**3\. Experimental Conditions**

Two well-controlled conditions are required to achieve a baseline. These map directly onto the previously described test positions.

**3.1 Condition 1: Finger-On-Tactor (FOT)**

Finger rests directly on the mechanical contact nipple of the tactor.

* True tactile stimulation is present.

* Electromagnetic noise from the tactor is present.

* EM noise from the environment is present

**3.2 Condition 2: IFNFN (Finger In Noise Field, Not Feeling Nipple)**

Finger placed in the same EM field generated by the tactor, without receiving tactile stimulation.

Implemented as one of the following equivalent configurations:

* Finger next to the contact nipple but still within the EM field.

* Finger on a mechanically identical tactor with the contact nipple removed or disabled.

This condition preserves EM noise while eliminating tactile input and is the **critical control condition**.

Only one factor may differ between conditions: **presence vs absence of tactile stimulation**.

Option: White noise headset (auditive stimulation)

Option: Visual stimulation (of the tractor without)

**Hardware filtering:**

* Hardware filters may be used for denoising signals *after* the Class D amplifier of VBS.

* Any hardware filter must be explicitly validated to ensure it does not distort amplitude, phase, timing, or relevant spectral content of the stim frequency.

**4.3 Two-Channel Considerations**

* Preferred setup uses two tactors:

  * FOT tactor (functional nipple)

  * IFNFN tactor (identical EM behavior, disabled/removed nipple)

* Assumes similar EM field emission; this must be verified empirically.

**5\. Positioning and Mechanical Control**

The finger position must be identical across conditions.

* Standard tractor: with or without tractor

  * Remove the nipple, double sided tape

* Use a fixed finger cradle, alignment marker, or 3D-printed finger bed.

Distance and orientation relative to the tactor must not vary.

Mechanical consistency is essential to ensure that the contrast isolates neural effects rather than positioning artifact

**6\. Test Procedure**

**6.1 Number of Cycles / Trials**

* Absolute minimum: 50 cycles

  * SNR changes with \* 7

* Target / best practice: 100 cycles per condition

  * SNR changes with \* 10

**6.2 Switching Schemes (in order of preference)**

1. **Randomized sequence** 

   1. Subject is prompted via screen to place the finger in the correct position.

   2. Minimizes expectancy and behavioural bias.

2. **Alternating sequence** (1 → 2 → 1 → 2\)

   1. Screen prompts required to maintain timing and pacing.

3. **Blocked batches**

   1. 20 cycles of condition 1, followed by 20 cycles of condition 2, repeated.

4. **Fully blocked**

   1. 100 cycles of condition 1, followed by 100 cycles of condition 2\.

   2. Least preferred, but currently the only option supported by the existing sweep software.

**7\. Timing and Triggering**

**7.1 Triggering**

* A software trigger marks the exact start of stimulation (t \= 0).

* Extreme sub-millisecond precision is not required (not an ERP paradigm).

* Known event timestamp bugs must remain fixed.

**7.2 Recommended Timing**

* Pre-stimulation baseline: ≥ 1 s

* Stimulation duration: \~4 s per trial

* Post-stimulation recording:

  * Optional, but **recommended** when studying resonant frequencies or post-stimulus effects.

**8\. Epoching Strategy**

Epochs are created separately for conditions 1 and 2\.

* Baseline window: −1.0 s to −0.5 s relative to STIM-ON

* Stimulation window: \+0.5 s to \+X.0 s

Notes:

* Data immediately around t \= 0 must be excluded due to filter and wavelet smearing.

* Trimming applies symmetrically before and after STIM-ON to ensure clean state separation.

**9\. Data Processing Pipeline (Time–Frequency Domain)**

**Step 1 — Full Time–Frequency Transform**

* Apply TFR (e.g. Morlet wavelets, MNE TFR) to the *entire* recording.

Output: channels × time × frequency.

**Step 2 — Epoching**

* Epoch around triggers using the windows defined above.

**Step 3 — Baseline Normalization**

* Use ratio, log-ratio, or dB normalization.

Purpose:

* Removes general EEG noise

* Suppresses slow drifts

* Reduces EM and environmental noise common to both conditions

**Step 4 — Contrasting**

Compute:

* Mean TFR(FOT)

* Mean TFR(IFNFN)

Then compute:

**Contrast \= FOT − IFNFN**

This cancels:

* EM noise from the tactor

* Environmental noise

* Non-task-related brain activity

* Variance caused by finger proximity

Result:

Isolated neural response attributable to tactile stimulation.

**13\. Frequency Selection**

* Frequencies must be selected conservatively due to the high number of cycles required.

* Avoid frequencies that are divisors of 50 Hz to minimize power-line contamination.

* Prioritize frequencies of strongest theoretical and physiological relevance.

**14\. Recommended Workflow Summary**

**Preparation**

* Build 2-channel setup (FOT \+ IFNFN tactors).

* Ensure fixed finger positioning.

* Define randomized or semi-randomized trial schedule.

**Recording**

* Software trigger at t \= 0\.

* Record baseline \+ stimulation \+ optional post-stimulus.

* Collect 50–100 trials per condition.

**Analysis**

* Compute TFR on full recording.

* Epoch around triggers.

* Apply baseline normalization.

* Average per condition.

* Compute FOT − IFNFN contrast.

The resulting signal represents the clean neural response to tactile stimulation under strong EM noise conditions.
